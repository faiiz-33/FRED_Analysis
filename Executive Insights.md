Executive Insights

Overview

This project analyzes long-term U.S. macroeconomic relationships using FRED data (1988–2024), focusing on inflation, unemployment, interest rates, and housing prices. Linear regression models and scenario analysis are used to understand structural relationships rather than produce short-term forecasts.

⸻

Key Findings

Inflation Dynamics
	•	Inflation shows a weak but negative relationship with unemployment, consistent with a flattened Phillips Curve.
	•	Using a 24-month interest rate lag, inflation responds more strongly to labor market conditions than in short-lag models.
	•	Interest rates exhibit a positive association with inflation, reflecting monetary policy reaction rather than causal tightening effects.

Inflation model (24-month lag):
	•	Unemployment coefficient: −0.147
	•	Interest rate coefficient: +0.176

⸻

Housing Market Behavior
	•	House prices increase substantially over the long run, with the index rising from ~60 (1988) to ~325 (2024).
	•	Housing markets are highly sensitive to inflation and strongly dampened by higher interest rates, with delayed effects.

House price inflation model (24-month lag):
	•	Inflation coefficient: +0.661
	•	Interest rate coefficient: −0.618

This confirms that:
	•	Inflationary environments strongly amplify housing price growth.
	•	Tight monetary policy effectively cools housing markets, but with a lag.

⸻

Crisis Periods and Structural Breaks
	•	The models successfully capture normal business-cycle dynamics, including the 2008 disinflation.
	•	They fail to capture the 2020–2022 inflation spike, highlighting a structural break driven by supply shocks and fiscal stimulus.
	•	These periods reinforce the need for scenario analysis rather than point forecasting.

⸻

Scenario Analysis Insights
	•	Holding unemployment constant, higher past interest rates are associated with slightly higher inflation, reflecting policy reaction.
	•	Holding interest rates constant, higher unemployment leads to lower inflation.
	•	For housing:
	•	Low rates + high inflation → strongest house price growth
	•	High rates offset inflation and significantly reduce house price inflation

Scenario analysis proves more informative than direct prediction for policy and strategic interpretation.

⸻

Conclusions
	•	Macroeconomic relationships are stable over long horizons, but break down during extreme shocks.
	•	Linear regression is best used here as an interpretive and scenario-based tool, not a forecasting engine.
	•	Housing markets amplify macroeconomic forces more strongly than consumer prices.
	•	Interest rates operate with long and asymmetric lags, particularly in housing.

⸻

Limitations
	•	Models do not capture expectations, fiscal policy, or supply constraints explicitly.
	•	Post-2020 dynamics represent a regime change outside the scope of linear specifications.

⸻

Final Takeaway

This analysis demonstrates how inflation, labor markets, monetary policy, and housing interact over time, providing structural insights and policy-relevant scenarios rather than short-term predictions.
